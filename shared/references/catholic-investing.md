# Catholic Moral Investing — Reference

## USCCB Socially Responsible Investment Guidelines (2021)

The foundational standard for Catholic institutional investors. First published 1991, major revision November 2021. Three-pronged strategy: Avoid Doing Harm, Actively Work for Change, Promote the Common Good.

### Five Categories of Exclusion

**I. Protecting Human Life**
- Abortion (direct involvement) — absolute exclusion
- Abortifacients
- Euthanasia and assisted suicide
- In vitro fertilization
- Human cloning
- Embryonic stem cell and fetal tissue research that ends life
- Contraceptives
- Gender reassignment (added 2021)

**II. Protecting Human Dignity**
- Pornography / adult entertainment
- Human rights abuses: child labor, forced labor
- Racial and gender discrimination
- Media/telecommunications that fail to promote human dignity (added 2021, includes social media)

**III. Enhancing the Common Good**
- Controversial weapons, arms production
- Nuclear weapons, land mines, cluster munitions
- Gambling (companies whose primary revenue derives from gambling)
- Tobacco (companies whose primary purpose is tobacco production)
- Recreational cannabis (added 2021)

**IV. Pursuing Economic Justice**
- Companies persistently violating workers' human rights (with engagement prior to divestment)
- Corruption and unfair business practices

**V. Saving Our Global Home (added 2021)**
- Practices leading to biodiversity loss and water degradation
- Integration of Laudato Si' principles on climate and the poor
- Fossil fuel engagement and potential divestment

### Active Ownership

- Shareholder advocacy through proxy voting
- Corporate dialogues with management
- Supporting shareholder resolutions
- Engagement before divestment on labor and environmental issues

## Knights of Columbus Asset Advisors (KoCAA)

$28.9 billion AUM. Applies full USCCB guidelines. Employs a full-time moral theologian. Created a Responsible Investing Steering Committee (RISC).

**Distinguishing features:**
- Broadest Catholic screen — covers all five USCCB categories
- ESG analysis integrated as a risk-mitigation tool alongside Catholic screens
- Active shareholder engagement through dialogues, proxy voting, and resolutions
- Guided by both USCCB Guidelines (2021) and Mensuram Bonam (2022)
- Explicitly notes that Catholic teaching and secular ESG diverge on some issues (e.g., abortion framed as "human rights" in secular ESG)

## Ave Maria Mutual Funds

$3.8 billion AUM. Largest Catholic-oriented investing firm in the US. Narrower screens focused specifically on pro-life and pro-family issues.

**The Four Moral Screens:**
1. Abortion — companies involved in the practice
2. Planned Parenthood — companies contributing corporate funds
3. Pornography — distribution or policies undermining the sacrament of marriage
4. Embryonic Stem Cell Research — companies conducting, servicing, or developing products from ESCR

**What Ave Maria does NOT exclude (unlike KoCAA/USCCB):**
- Defense companies
- Alcohol
- Tobacco
- Gambling

**Catholic Advisory Board (CAB):** Sets all moral screening criteria. Guided by the Magisterium. Independent of the Roman Catholic Church — not officially authorized.

**Impact on investable universe:** Approximately 5% of the Russell 3000 excluded. Minimal practical impact on diversification.

## Catholic Social Teaching Principles

### Dignity of the Human Person
Every person has inherent dignity from being made in God's image. Never profit from activities that violate human dignity. Not a preference — an absolute moral prohibition.

### Universal Destination of Goods
"The first principle of the economic order" (John Paul II). All creation is given to all of humanity. Private property is not absolute but a means to this universal destination. Wealth carries moral obligations.

### Subsidiarity
Decisions belong at the smallest effective organizational level. Support local economies, microfinance, community development. Resist excessive centralization of economic power.

### Solidarity
"A firm, persevering determination to commit oneself to the common good" (John Paul II). Active engagement with companies on labor, supply chain, community impact. Shareholder advocacy as duty, not optional.

### Preferential Option for the Poor
Economic decisions evaluated by impact on the most vulnerable. Direct capital toward affordable housing, underserved communities. Evaluate environmental impacts through the lens of who suffers most.

### Stewardship of Creation
The earth is entrusted for responsible care, not exploitation. Environmental screens, climate engagement, avoidance of biodiversity destruction. Rooted in Laudato Si' (2015).

## Key Documents

- **USCCB Socially Responsible Investment Guidelines (2021)** — the de facto US Catholic standard
- **Mensuram Bonam (2022)** — Pontifical Academy of Social Sciences; 24 categories of exclusion; "engage, enhance, and exclude" in that order; may signal future authoritative document
- **Caritas in Veritate (2009)** — Benedict XVI; "every economic decision has a moral consequence"
- **Laudato Si' (2015)** — Francis; stewardship of creation, climate, impact on the poor
- **Veritatis Splendor (1993)** — John Paul II; "it is not licit to do evil that good may come of it"

## The Return Question

Catholic-screened portfolios have historically matched or exceeded broad market returns:
- Christian Investment Forum: faith-based equity funds returned 7.1% annualized vs 6.3% benchmark over 15 years
- Parametric Portfolio Associates: Catholic values portfolios achieve market-like performance
- Ave Maria: only ~5% of Russell 3000 excluded

The Catholic intellectual response to "fiduciary duty requires maximizing returns":
1. The premise that investment decisions can be morally neutral is rejected. Moral neutrality is itself a moral claim — and a false one.
2. Certain acts are intrinsically evil. Profiting from them is doing evil that good may come of it. Never permissible regardless of financial outcome.
3. "No investment of money is morally neutral" (Mensuram Bonam). Either God's kingdom is being advanced or undermined.
4. Fiduciary duty includes moral stewardship — it is not limited to return maximization.
5. The empirical evidence is a bonus, not the foundation. Screens are morally required regardless of return impact.
